Lights, Dreams, and City Streets in the City That Never Sleeps

About New York City

New York City is one of the most iconic cities in the world. It's made up of five boroughs: Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, The Bronx, and Staten Island.
The city is famous for its towering skyscrapers like the Empire State Building, the Statue of Liberty, and Central Park — a massive green oasis in the middle of the urban jungle.
NYC is also the most linguistically diverse city on Earth, with over 800 languages spoken.
It's a global hub for finance, fashion, art, and entertainment, and it truly never sleeps.

Statue of Liberty located on Liberty Island in New York Harbor, it was a gift from France in 1886 to celebrate freedom and democracy.
Standing 93 meters tall, the statue holds a torch and a tablet marked July 4, 1776 — the date of American independence.
Designed by Frederic Auguste Bartholdi and engineered by Gustave Eiffel, it remains a global symbol of hope, liberty, and welcome.

Central Park is the green heart of New York City.
Located in the middle of Manhattan, it covers 3.4 square kilometers and offers a peaceful escape from the busy streets.
The park was designed in the 19th century and includes lakes, bridges, gardens, and even a castle.
Famous landmarks inside the park include Bethesda Fountain, Bow Bridge, and the Central Park Zoo.
Every year, millions of visitors come to enjoy its beauty in every season.

Times Square is one of the most famous and vibrant places in New York City.
Located at the intersection of Broadway and 7th Avenue, it's known for its massive digital billboards, bright lights, and nonstop energy.
The square got its name in 1904 when The New York Times moved its headquarters there.
Today, it's a major center for entertainment, shopping, and tourism.
At midnight on New Year's Eve, the famous ball drop takes place here, marking the start of the new year in a dazzling celebration.

Brooklyn Bridge Opened in 1883, it connects Manhattan and Brooklyn over the East River.
At the time of its completion, it was the longest suspension bridge in the world.
Designed by John A. Roebling and completed by his son Washington Roebling, the bridge features neo-Gothic towers and steel cables.
Today, it's a popular spot for walking, biking, and sightseeing, offering stunning views of the city skyline and the Statue of Liberty.
